WiFi Pineapple Pager

The WiFi Pineapple Pager by Hak5 is a portable device designed for wireless audits: its goal is to monitor the environment and notify relevant changes or events so you can decide when it’s worth performing deeper analysis with full tooling.

What it is

In practice, the Pager works as an early‑warning system for Wi‑Fi (and, depending on configuration, Bluetooth/BLE). It is not meant to replace a full lab setup. It aims to reduce operational friction in the field, when you can’t keep your eyes on a console all the time.

Capabilities (summary)

  • Tri‑band Wi‑Fi (2.4 / 5 / 6 GHz), focused on reconnaissance and monitoring.
  • Bluetooth / BLE observation (depending on workflow and configuration).
  • Internal battery and USB‑C charging for portable use.
  • Alerts via on‑device feedback and notifications (as configured).
  • Virtual Pager: a web interface to view status and review events more comfortably.

How it works

  1. Observe: detects signals and changes in the air (SSIDs, variations, presence, etc.).
  2. Classify: applies rules/thresholds to separate noise from actionable events.
  3. Notify: triggers an alert when a defined condition is met.
  4. Verify: the operator validates and escalates to full capture/log tools when needed.

Virtual Pager (web interface)

The Virtual Pager lets you operate the device through a web interface. It’s useful to mirror the screen live, review events more comfortably, and treat the Pager as a “sensor” while the laptop handles deeper analysis when required.

Field use: ergonomics and workflow

The Pager makes the most sense when mobility matters. For on‑site audits, quick validations, and walk‑throughs, the value is knowing when something changes without interrupting the work.

Pricing and alternatives (Raspberry Pi)

This is where the comparison becomes relevant. The Pager typically sits around €300+ (approx., depending on store/taxes), while a reasonable Raspberry Pi setup with accessories can be closer to ~€160 (approx., depending on components). The key difference is not only cost, but time: a Pi gives you flexibility; the Pager aims for a more direct experience.

Pros and cons

Pros

  • Mobility: designed for field use.
  • Alerts: reduces constant screen watching.
  • Virtual Pager: improves operation and event review.

Cons

  • Configuration‑dependent: poor tuning can generate noise.
  • Not a full platform: you still need analysis tooling for evidence.
  • Price: competes with DIY options that can cover part of the role.

Ethics note

Use these tools only on your own environments or with explicit permission. Unauthorised auditing is not research—it’s a problem.

Verdict

The Pager is valuable when used as a portable early‑warning device within a disciplined workflow: define what matters, filter, and use it to decide when deeper analysis is justified. If you need a fully flexible platform, a DIY approach (such as a Raspberry Pi) may be a better fit—at the cost of time and maintenance.
